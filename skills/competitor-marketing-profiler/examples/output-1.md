# Example 1 — Output

---

**Your brand:** Byoma — barrier-focused skincare built around a clinically-backed ingredient philosophy, positioned at the credible-affordable intersection.
**Competitor:** Bubble Skincare — Gen Z-first skincare brand leading with self-expression and community, with a strong retail presence.
**Primary audience assumed:** Gen Z and younger Millennials, first-time skincare buyers, barrier-health aware.
**Market scope:** US primary, English-language markets.

---

## Section 1 — Who They Are

| | Byoma | Bubble Skincare |
|---|---|---|
| Positioning | Science-backed barrier care at an accessible price point | Self-expression through skincare, built with and for Gen Z |
| Primary claim | Clinically formulated to support skin barrier health | Real products for real skin, made with teens in mind |
| Implied audience | Informed first-timer who wants to feel they made the smart choice | Teen and early-20s buyer who wants to feel seen and represented |
| Tone | Calm, credible, ingredient-educated | Energetic, playful, community-first |

These two brands are going after the same price tier and roughly the same age group, but with meaningfully different positioning logic. Byoma is making a credibility play — the buyer they want feels good because they made an informed, science-backed decision. Bubble is making an identity play — their buyer feels good because the brand understands who they are. Byoma wins with the buyer who's done a little research; Bubble wins with the buyer who finds the brand through a friend, a TikTok, or a Walmart shelf.

- **Where they overlap:** Both target the affordable-but-credible skincare tier for younger buyers concerned about skin health
- **Where they diverge:** Byoma leads with ingredient science; Bubble leads with community and self-expression — different emotional contracts with the same demographic
- **Consistency:** Bubble holds consistent across all surfaces — website, packaging, and social feel like the same brand. Byoma is tighter on the website than on social, where the barrier-science angle softens noticeably

## Section 3 — How They Think About Their Audience

**Bubble Skincare**
- **Who they're writing for:** A teenager or early-20s buyer who wants to feel like the brand was made specifically for them
- **What that person worries about:** Not being represented; products that weren't designed with their skin or budget in mind
- **Sophistication assumed:** Low — they lead with feelings and outcomes, not ingredients or formulation
- **Primary lever:** Belonging — "this is your brand, these are your people"
- **Evidence:** Heavy UGC usage, teenage creator collabs, community-forward copy across every surface

**Byoma**
- **Who they're writing for:** A first-timer who has done a little research and wants to feel they made the informed, smart choice
- **What that person worries about:** Wasting money on products that don't work, not understanding what ingredients actually do
- **Sophistication assumed:** Moderate to high — clinical language appears more readily than at most brands in this price range
- **Primary lever:** Confidence in the decision — this buyer wants to feel smart, not just included
- **Evidence:** Barrier health terminology, ingredient callouts, and formulation specificity that most affordable skincare brands actively avoid

These brands could be writing for the same person at different moments — Bubble wins the identity-led first purchase, Byoma wins the research-backed repurchase. Bubble currently owns the first touchpoint more effectively because their social presence and retail positioning are built for discovery; Byoma has stronger credibility once someone is looking, but isn't doing enough to get found first.

## Section 5 — Strategic Takeaways

**🟢 Pursue**
- **Build on YouTube with ingredient education content.** Bubble has no presence there and the audience Byoma targets actively watches this format — it directly reinforces the credibility positioning that already works on the website.
- **Own the maturing skincare buyer explicitly.** Create content and messaging that speaks to someone graduating from entry-level routines — Bubble's teen-coded brand can't follow without contradicting itself.
- **Develop proof-backed ingredient content on TikTok and Instagram.** Bubble avoids ingredient specifics entirely; Byoma can own the "what's actually in your skincare and why it matters" content lane without a direct fight.

**🛡️ Defend**
- **Don't compete on social posting volume.** Bubble's frequency and UGC scale is a distribution advantage built over time — competing on output is expensive and doesn't play to Byoma's strengths. Invest in quality and specificity instead.
- **Reframe the retail gap as a positioning asset.** Being DTC-forward can be positioned as "made for people who actually research what they buy" — lean into it rather than treating it as a weakness.

**👁️ Watch**
- **Bubble adding ingredient transparency.** If Bubble decides to build a credibility layer on top of their community positioning, it closes the most important gap Byoma currently holds — worth monitoring for any messaging shift in that direction.
- **TikTok algorithm changes affecting ingredient education content.** Byoma's strongest organic channel could shift if educational content is deprioritized — diversifying into YouTube and SEO while TikTok is working is a hedge worth considering.
